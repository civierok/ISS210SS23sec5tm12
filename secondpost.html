Racial Segregation Among Schools Within Districts

How racialized markers segregate children from low income families into separate schools; leading to poor education.

Posted by Shae Buroker on April 28, 2023

Children in low income areas seem to always be left behind. In recent years, it has been evident that marginalized groups seem to be separated from white class peers due to unfair financing in the schooling system.

Being raised in low income households has an effect on speech, learning and emotional development. Without these skills and education acquired from proper schooling, it is difficult for these children to be set up for success. They are less likely to be hired for well-paying jobs, adversely hindering their ability to contribute to the economy.

According to an article done by Sage Journals, over ⅕ of kindergarten students were from Latino heritage in 17 states. A theory called place stratification states that class and racial markers continue to leave Latinos in subordinate and isolated positions, even in promising destinations.

“Economic mobility is no guarantee of residential integration,” state scholars Lichter, Parisi, and Taquino (2015).

This proves that race plays a role in placement of schools. Even with schools becoming more diverse, economic and racial factors will continue to segregate those of the Latino heritage into separate schools.

Lend a Helping Hand

This puts these children in an inevitable cycle of being put at a disadvantage. They are the future of our country and we must integrate them into communities that will allow them to receive proper education without regressive allocation of teachers and a culturally limited curriculum. Supporting things like non-profit organizations are a great way to help the community of those in need. These children did not choose to be born into this life, so why make them suffer more.
